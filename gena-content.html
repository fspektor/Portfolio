    Gena: Inclusive Sex-Ed Toolkit
    Gena is an open source sex-ed toolkit for the blind & visually impaired. We are creating these inclusive anatomical models for use in low-resourced sex education settings.

    The call for this project was originally brought to us by Laura Millar, a blind sex education leader and prolific Bay Area community organizer. We've since continued the work with the Tecnológico de Monterrey, and recieved support from the Jacobs Institute for Design Innovation. This is an ongoing work in progress, developed in collaboration with Annalise Kamegawa and Hailey Windsor.

    Defining Our Problem Space
    If you're a blind or visually impaired (BVI) student, chances are you've been shorted on comprehensive sex education. While sighted students have access to photos and illustrations, BVI students have limited means to recieve equivalent information.

    Though a few tactile tools exist, the cost for educators to acquire them is often prohibitive.

    Research & Previous Work
    To tackle this problem, we referenced a variety of existing BVI educational materials, and talked to experts in the field.

    How we might create anatomical models that could benefit both visually impaired and sighted students?


    When I have models that make sense to folks, so many of the other topics can be built on top of that basic information. So I think a great focus should be on basic anatomy and adding some variations.

    — Laura Millar, Community Organizer and Sex Education Activist

    Our Concept
    As an entry point on the project, we are creating an "exploded view" of female anatomy that educators can cheaply and easily fabricate themselves.

    The models will be able to support braille descriptions, as well as audio buttons. Students will be able to explore internal and external anatomy by peeling back layers of differing textures.

    Too many assistive devices for disability depend on advanced technologies, often overshooting the needs of their users. To this end, we want the processes of fabrication to be simple and understandable; anyone should be able to recreate a version of this model with the resources they have on hand.

    Testing with the Community
    We have recently undergone several rounds of testing with material prototypes with BVI users, and are continuing to make improvements.

    We are also working with educators from the BVI community to produce educational modules that go along with the model. These currently include lessons on reproductive health, such as how tampons are inserted and how various contraceptives work; pleasure activism; and gender-diversity.

    Plans for Distribution
    & Next Steps
    By splitting the toolkit into 1) the model base, 2) tactile components, and 3) electronic components, we consider what the model may look like in a variety of contexts. In order to optimize our project’s accessibility, we are designing a website that will host files for each of these layers.

    Using our carefully designed templates, we hope a low fidelity version made with a cardboard base and paper mache tactile components will prove as useful a high fidelity version made with molded silicone and electronics.

    Rather than build a single product, our approach to this project prioritizes an iterative pipeline of templates and sex-ed resources to achieve our goals of inclusion.
